Created by Michael R. Wolf on 2019-10-09

As Sent by Tam

How You Say It

It is not so much what you say, as the
manner in which you say it.
It is not so much the words you use, as
the tone in which you convey it.
Come here! I sharply said, and the baby
cowered and wept;
Come here! I cooded; he looked and smiled
and to my lap he crept.
The words may be mild and fair, and the
tone may pierce like a dart;
The words may be soft as the summer air
and the tones may break the heart.
Whether you know it or not, whether you
mean or care
Gentleness, kindness, love or hate; envy
and anger are there.
If you would quarrels avoid, and in
peace and love rejoice;
Keep anger not only out of your words,
but keep it out of your voice.

-- Author Unknown

Correcting purported authorship of Anisha Sipporah

At https://www.poemhunter.com/poem/tone-of-voice/, I left this comment:

This poem is over 100 years old.

A quick search (with some of the original lines) will find references to various versions of the poem, especially the first stanza. It is known both as "How You Say It" and "Tone of Voice". The poem is attributed to "Author Unknown", not to Anisha Sipporah.

https://www.google.com/search?tbm=bks&hl=en&q="It+is+not+so+much+what+you+say%2C+as+the+manner+in+which+you+say+it"

The majority of the references seem to be from 1905-1910, and from 1890-1900. Early publication with author unknown seems to have had a revival at the turn of the previous century in the journals and magazines of various temperance, christian, professional or fraternal organizations.

Here's one such reference:
"Sunday School Helper", Vol XXIII, January 1892. Harvard Divinity School.
