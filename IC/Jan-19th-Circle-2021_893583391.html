HOW TO SAY WHAT YOU DO
in one compelling sentence

The Tender Art of Niching

The Simple Idea

The most compelling topic for anyone is themselves.

Instead of describing what you do or who you are,
your One Compelling Sentence describes
who your client is and what they struggle with.
There will be plenty of time later to explain how you help them.


Calling to them - Wendy/Smiley - hard to not turn your head when name is called
WAIT - you get me? you get my struggle? hard to not pay attention

The Teaching

Most people define themselves and their business by their label:
“I’m a coach.” “I’m a mortgage broker.” “I’m a massage therapist.”
Or, they define themselves by their process:
“I do life coaching.” “I do marketing.” “I do massage.”

In this

- the focus is entirely on you.

- Plus, having the focus on you promotes stage fright,
and this isn't a performance. Ideally, you just get to be yourself.

-you are killing creativity and flexibility in your business right from the get-go.
What if you want to expand your skills or your roles?
In adding a new label or process, are you really needing to create a whole new business?


People are in need and really do want your help.
But, they are so focused on their own challenges
that they don’t have a lot of time and attention for you.

If you throw your label or process in front of them,
you are a distraction, getting between them and their goal.

However, if you put your One Compelling Sentence in front of them,
instead of standing between them and their goal,
you are suddenly standing shoulder to shoulder with them,
helping them to reach their goal.

They are going to be very interested in you.

Very compelling!
And it can leave you feeling tender.
Kinda Visible...

The tenderness arises when you let go of the safety in this statement:
“I’m here to work with whoever asks me to help them.”
In that statement you don’t risk rejection.
You don’t risk anything,
except not serving forks, because so few people see that your business is for them.

The shift from the reactive, “I’ll work with you if you ask,”
to the proactive, “I’m here to help these people confronting this problem,”
is a remarkable, passionate, caring, powerful stance to take.

People will notice this.
Actually, they’ll be more certain you are the one who really understands them and can help.
Compelling, isn’t it?
And tender.

They are Vulnerable to share with us, to say their challenge, to allow our help.
It is useful to be tender and vulnerable ourselves.

That choice, once made,
will bring you those wonderful things you’re yearning for:
finding your clients,
more powerful and compelling marketing, and
successfully receiving referrals,
as well as ease in creating offers,
among other things.

And, of course, clarity and ease in answering the question
“So... what do you do?”

We can’t help everyone.

Therefore, confronted with this question:
who do you want to help?
And who aren’t you here to help?

The humility required here is the trust that everyone and everything is in the hands of the Divine.
That you are not the sole safety net for the universe.

This Means Fun

!!! The people you most enjoy working with are the people you are meant to serve.!!!

Who drinks up what you have and eagerly asks for more?
Who are your best clients, who do you do your best work with?

Permission and Surrender

“NO!” is what the Divine says to all that isn’t Truth.

Lets say NO to Who/What is not ours
so can make room for bigger YES for Who/What is

Exercise

  1. Ask your heart: who is your business really here for?
    Who are you really here for? As you get any little bits of clarity, write it down. Breathe. Relax. Open.

Note: This is NOT your One Compelling Sentence, or even marketing language of any sort.
This is just for you.
So ask, trust your soul, and see what comes. Say “Yes” to whatever comes.


The surprisingly tender process of shifting from working with whoever shows up
to planting your flag and saying “I’m here for these people!”
is made easier simply through accepting your heart’s truth.

The Problem:
Any time someone is stuck and unable to progress with some task, project or goal.


The Simple Idea

Name what someone is struggling with and you have their full attention.

Whatever problem people face,
wherever your best clients get stuck,
it tends to take up all of their attention.

Giving them empathy for that problem,
letting them know you see them there,
is extremely compelling.
You’ll have their full attention.

The Teaching

Did you notice how these referrals happened?
Somebody mentioned a problem. Fever and cough. Trouble communicating. Challenging teenager.
These problems triggered an association for the listener, who made the referral without thinking about it.

Many people in business insist on marketing their solution,
and try to become known for what they do.
If you notice, neither of the referrals made any mention of how the problem would be solved.
Just that the problem would be solved.

But hey, you might say,
“I don’t want to be associated with a problem. I don’t want to focus on the negative.”

Surprisingly, associating with a problem
can be a much more rewarding spiritual state than associating with a solution.
One of our most basic needs is to feel seen, loved, appreciated, known, valued.

If I’m hurt, I need that hurt acknowledged and addressed
before I have any space to hear about your solution.

If the people you are helping are in pain or discomfort,
you have to name it to gain their trust and their attention.

It’s been shown repeatedly that mentioning a problem
gets 10 times the response from prospective customers over mentioning a solution.
And when you are speaking the problem with sincere empathy, it’s deeply healing.
.

Look for One Problem

There are two ways to go about it.

  1. You can look for an uber-problem, or top-level problem, that captures all the other problems.
    For instance, a naturopath may work with Lymes disease, Lupus, and other related issues,
    but all of which fall under “struggles with the immune system.”

  2. You can look for a fundamental problem that doesn’t cover every- thing, but it’s something the vast majority of your best clients struggle with.
    For instance, the fundamental problem for the naturpath above could be
    “completely worn out from side effects from Western drugs and not really getting better from their chronic illness.”
    The naturopath could be working with people struggling with all kinds of chronic illnesses,
    but the fundamental issue has to do with their experience
    with ineffective treatments they’ve been receiving from their medical doctors.


Say It How They Say It

How do your clients describe the problem?
Classic disparity is an acupuncturist who says, “Stagnant chi”
and the client who says, “Feels miserable.”

Say it how the client says it:
Overwhelmed, too much to do.
website doesn’t work.

.


Problem: Any time someone is stuck and unable to progress with some task, project or goal.

A Problem Doesn’t Have to Be Life-Threatening
Don’t underestimate the problem you solve.
Don’t try to make it deeper or worse, or more serious.
We are all in need of lightness and beauty and fun.
Our struggles, even if they aren’t life-threatening, matter to us in the moment.


Exercise

  1. Now’s the time to just make a big list. What problems do your clients struggle with

that what you do helps in some fashion?
Don’t edit it, don’t shorten it, don’t make it “realistic” or “marketing-ese.”
Just list’em.
As much as possible, use language that your clients would use to describe the problem.
Think about what they’ve told you they want help with, or what they have complained about.
Can you remember the exact words and phrases they’ve used?

Also, please don’t get too humble.
While it’s true that no one can really fix anyone else,
you can, and do, assist/support/help them with whatever they are struggling with.
Own that.
If you’re new, it’s okay. You still help.
And, with time, you’ll help more.

2. Look over your list and narrow it down, if necessary.
Which Problems are relevant in particular to the work you do?
You are looking either for
1) a problem that captures the essence or overview of all the other particular problems, or
2) a fundamental or foundational problem all your clients struggle with in some way,
even though they have other problems you help, too.

.

The Simple Idea Revisited

People are yearning to be witnessed, without judgement, in their struggles.
Empathy is extremely compelling.

If you can name the problem someone is struggling with, in her own words, she’ll feel seen.
And if she believes you “get” the struggle she’s having,
she will also be more likely to believe that whatever solution you are proposing
will actually help her get through the struggle.
