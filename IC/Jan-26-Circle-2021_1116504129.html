How To Say What You Do In One Compelling Sentence

The Simple Idea

The three elements make a surprisingly compelling sentence for your right clients.

Collect/ write yours now (if don’t know, make it up)
The Demographic -
the Psychographic -
the Problem -

If you have more than one of any of them, that’s okay.
List them Now - paper or computer
This is just the beginning, so just dance with this.

Your three elements, the Demographic, the Psychographic, and The Problem. 
All put together into a sentence are an extraodinarily compelling combination,
for all the reasons that each element is so compelling on its own.


The Teaching

Finally, The formula for your One Compelling Sentence.

Share and review:

***
One Compelling Sentence - construct using those elements:

“I help DEMOGRAPHIC PSYCHOGRAPHIC people who struggle with THE PROBLEM.”
or it could look like:
“I help DEMOGRAPHIC who care about PSYCHOGRAPHIC who are struggling with THE PROBLEM.”

I help (support, equip, inspire) [Demographic] 
who want (care about, need, struggle with, desire, are committed-to) [Psychographic] 
Who STRUGGLE WITH (WANT, NEED, are tired of, who suffer from) [The Problem.]”

If you have more than one word/concept for Demographic, Psychographic, or Problem, write 3-5 different versions, and keep playing with it:

Example: 
Demographic: Older women. 
Psychographics: Fun/Play, authenticity and contribution, 
The Problem: “Have the blahs.”

- I help older women who love play who struggle with the blahs. 
- I help older women who want to make a contribution and who are tired of the blahs. 
- I help older women with the blahs who struggle to feel truly authentic. 
- I help older women who want to be their playful selves and yet feel stuck in the blahs.

***
Meditation:

“I help DEMOGRAPHIC PSYCHOGRAPHIC people who struggle with THE PROBLEM.”

It’s the truth of what you do. 
You help people. 
You help certain people with a certain problem. 

When someone asks you what you do, you say,
“I help these people with this problem.” 

You can mix it up.
You can change the order of the elements. 
You can get comfy with the sentence.

What’s really important is that you are extremely clear in your own mind and soul 
what those three elements are. 

.
When you are, 
you can say the sentence however you like 
and it won’t sound scripted, 
and it will be surprisingly effective.

It’s almost understated. 
But don’t be deceived, it is extraordinarily powerful.


.

Two Words You Want to Include

There are two words to include in your sentence 
which make a subtle and powerful shift 
in the effectiveness of your sentence. 

Here they are: “Who want” 
(alternatives: “who need” or “who struggle with”). 

**
Example for Two Words You Want to Include “Who want” 
(alternatives: “who need” or “who struggle with”):

Instead of saying “I help women struggling with chronic illness get their life back,” 

say, “I help women struggling with chronic illness who want to get their life back.”

**

Try this: say the first sentence out loud and notice what happens in your body & soul.

Then try saying the second sentence out loud. Notice a difference?

The first one has the subtle message of “This is what it is.” The “who want” 

in the second sentence brings a subtle energetic invitation, opening, spaciousness.

In the first sentence you’re telling them what you’re going to do to/for these women. 

The second sentence talks about the desires, the hopes, the goals of these women, 

it keeps the focus entirely on them. 


This is THEIR PROBLEM, Their Creation, NOT Yours
You CAN’t FIX it - you CAN Assist, IF they Choose


ALSO

“Who want” preserves your integrity. 

If you’re doing any kind of transformational work, 
you know it’s very hard, and not in integrity, 
to make some kinds of promises. 

Without the “who want” you’re making a promise to “get their life back.” 
By saying “who want” you’re simply saying you help people who want to get their lives back, 
or whatever they are wanting. 

You aren’t making an outrageous promise. 
It’s more believable, 
and you stay in integrity.

Don’t Talk About What You Do

It is extremely, almost irresistibly tempting
to slip some statement about what you do or how you do what you do 
into your One Compelling Sentence. Don’t do it!

For instance, “I help women with chronic illness who really want a normal life again.” 
That’s a simple, powerful sentence. 
If you include “I use hypnotherapy to help…” 
you ruin the effect. 

Why?

Whatever you do, people have pictures or assumptions about it. 

If you drop it in the beginning, 
instead of later in the conversation once you’re already engaged, 
they will be reacting to their previous experience or belief about whatever it is you do, 
not you yourself. 

If they had a mediocre or bad experience with hypnotherapy, they’ll tune you out.
it’s going to be easy to slip into talking about your process, method, approach. 

Restrain yourself. Retrain yourself.

You can talk about how you help them later, after they start asking questions.

The Other Way To Say It, “You know how…”

This second way is actually longer than a sentence, 

it’s more like two or three sentences. 

But it has a very compelling story-like construction, 

and it sounds very conversational, unscripted, and disarming.

It’s a very compelling story in two or three sentences. 

And the “who want” also plays an important additional role in this version


***

Example: 

the “You know how” construction goes like this:


“You know how so many women in their thirties, forties or beyond 

are struggling with chronic illnesses of all sorts? 

And how dispiriting, exhausting it is for their lives to be consumed by their illness, 

missing out on all the normal things they used to love to do? 

I help women like this who really want to get their life back.”
