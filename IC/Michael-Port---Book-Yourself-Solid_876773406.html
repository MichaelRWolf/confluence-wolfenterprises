Recommendation

The owners and employees of small service companies often end up doing it all, providing the service or product, managing the office, and handling sales and marketing - often the entrepreneur‚Äôs least favorite chore. Now, you can learn to love it or at least to do it well with less pain. Take-Aways

  • Get clients by becoming an authority, and by being likeable and trustworthy.

The "Red Velvet Rope Policy"

To begin the process of becoming completely booked by clients, build your foundation. First, weed out any current clients who drain your energy and lead to more stress than enjoyment. It‚Äôs scary to release clients when you need money, but having a red velvet rope policy - like the bouncer at an exclusive club - leads to getting "booked solid." Review which clients you love working with and why. The clients in the ideal category will help you define your specific target market.

"Finding a Target Market"

Having a defined audience makes it easier to locate clients who need what you offer. For instance, acupuncturists serve anyone in pain, but one acupuncturist narrowed his specialty to infertility. Rather than targeting everyone seeking pain relief, he focused on helping infertile couples. Once you build a strong client list in one specialty, you can add another target later. While trying to get clients in a profitable field sounds logical, it isn‚Äôt a good strategy unless you are also passionate about the work. When you‚Äôre not excited about your market, boredom sets in and drains away your energy. Get to know yourself. Identify the areas that excite you. Perhaps you can turn a hobby into a vocation. ‚ÄúMarketing and sales isn‚Äôt about trying to convince, coerce or manipulate people into buying your services. It‚Äôs about putting yourself out in front of, and offering your services to, those whom you are meant to serve - people who already need and are looking for your services.‚Äù

After identifying your target market, determine its requirements and desires. This will tell you why these clients need your services and what they want. Then show people the reward they will receive for becoming your clients. Define their return. Clients "technically" buy acupuncture, Web site design or coaching, but they "actually" buy financial freedom, security and free time. These benefits are emotional and personal. If you can reach your target market by touching these chords, you will boost your ability to draw clients. Next, build a personal brand, which denotes your identity and your position in your market.

You, the Personal Brand

A personal brand covers who you are, what you do and why you do it. Your personal brand is based on which problems you solve and for whom, why people should come to you, and what results your solutions produce. Identify the factors that make you different or even eccentric. If you struggle with this question, ask your close friends and colleagues to identify your unique personality traits and talents. Establishing Trust¬†

Start building credibility by ensuring that you have the core requirements in place to make your target customer feel confident about working with you. The basics are:

  • "High-quality service" - Clients will stay with you if you provide excellent service.¬†

  • Good "methods and tools" - Customers expect you to have a good process in place.¬†

  • Solid credentials - Qualifications and certification are most critical for service providers in specific roles such as doctors, lawyers and accountants. Sales Cycle Process

During the sales cycle, a prospect decides whether or not to buy from you. To ensure that your sales cycle process works, you must know your target market and what it needs. Be sure that your targets know where to find you, when to reach you, why they should select you and how to hire you. This step-by-step approach draws prospects in with a free offering, which encourages them next to buy an inexpensive product (thus creating some passive income for you), and gradually deepens their relationship with your business. Your goal is to give clients incentives and reasons to turn to your solutions more and more over time. To fulfill that objective, follow these steps in the sales cycle:

  1. Meet prospects - Carry out some activity daily to meet potential clients.

  2. "Always have something to invite people to" - Connect with potential clients by giving them risk-free samples. This relieves sales pressure, since you‚Äôre "inviting" the client to watch a free demo or download a free report.   3. Follow up - Keep building trust by taking a step beyond the earlier freebie. Offer prospects an inexpensive but valuable product or service that they‚Äôre ready to buy.

  4. Go beyond expectations - If, for instance, the client in step three bought a book, send the client an invitation to an event. If the client can‚Äôt attend, provide useful notes. Now, the client is deep into the sales cycle. Each step requires a bigger commitment.

  5. Repeat as needed - Carry out step four again, with a higher-level product or service.

Vary the number of steps in your sales cycle depending on your situation. The point is to keep providing value to clients at each stage, so they can decide if you‚Äôre right for them. Create a Product or Program

The time you spend creating a promotional product or program is worthwhile if you use it to reach as many people as possible. For example, publishing an audio CD or posting an Internet download about your area of expertise establishes you as an authority and markets your business. If you make an audio available online, prospects can access it any time. They might decide to buy your product or service based just on your audio, so you could make sales without doing anything else. After all, you already put in your time when you created and published a top-notch audio or download.

Seven Self-Promotion Strategies

To attract clients, spread the word about your expertise and abilities. Disseminate your message through as many different channels as possible. Solidify your foundation and your strategy for building trust and credibility before you implement any of these seven outreach strategies:

  1. "Networking Strategy" - Networking doesn‚Äôt necessarily mean making sales pitches, and racing to collect and hand out business cards.   2. "Web Strategy" - A Web site is a must-have to draw new clients. Your Web site should encourage visitors to act. Enable them to call, send an e-mail or enter contact information. Entice visitors to provide data by offering a free report, seminar or tutorial.   3. "Writing Strategy" - Build your expertise and credibility by submitting articles to e-mail newsletters, online "e-zines" and Web sites, as well as standard publications. If you find writing too stressful, hire a ghostwriter. Write about your passion for an area of your field. Tie your articles to your business. Give readers valuable information. If you publish online, include a short biography with a link to your Web site. Offer a resource box that prompts the reader to do something, such as join your mailing list, request more information or sign up for a free event.

  4. "Speaking/Demonstrating Strategy" - Use the podium to get your message out and find the people you‚Äôre meant to serve. You aren‚Äôt limited to standing in front of an audience. Offer free teleclasses. Seek events where you can talk to as many people as possible. Pursue opportunities to conduct demonstrations, start clubs or hold monthly lunches to build your reputation as someone who helps people connect with each other. This builds trust and credibility.

  5. "Referral Strategy" - Who gave you your last referral? What did the client need? What was the outcome? Use this information to determine why that approach worked so you can boost your referral traffic. Ask for more referrals. Try to get names and contact information so you can follow up.

  6. "Direct Outreach Strategy" - The tools you can use to reach clients include letters, e-mails, calls, newsletters, postcards or press releases, as well any other creative tactics you devise.   7. "Follow-Up Strategy" - Maintain contact on a regular basis by using such tools as e-mail newsletters, printed newsletters, postcards or phone calls. E-mail newsletters provide a simple, low-cost way to give readers information while advertising your products and services. The 80/20 rule works here: a successful newsletter uses 80% of its space for content, and 20% for advertising and promotion.
