Sacred Selling

we want an offer that practically sell itself.

Lets think about your offers differently, more creatively.

Once you get past thinking in terms of “sessions” or “widgets”
your offer can expand in very interesting ways


If you have enough experience in your business,
where you’ve seen enough clients arrive at points that they are happy about,
then it’s going to be easier to name these destinations.

But that’s not always the case, even if you have experience.
SO lets explore destinations.

WAIT_ But the Healing & Growing & IMPACT Journey Is Too Vague to Set a Destination

Many healers, transformation facilitators of all stripes,
take issue with the idea of “destination”

  • people’s journeys are so individual, and so magical,
    who knows where they’ll end up?

  • When someone has undergone very traumatic circumstances,
    it’s hard to define something like “inner peace” or “a sense of well-being.”
    They don’t Grok it - it is a concept, not an experience


Acknowledged. Yes. You can’t do that.
Lets concede this point, and honor it.

It’s a sacred, amazing, miraculous journey people take
and who knows where they’ll end up?

But! lets not chuck the destination piece.
Lets think differently

The internal process of healing is individual.

But what triggers the healing process?
What happens is that one day a person is trying to get somewhere in their life,
and –bam– they run into a roadblock.

That’s right, their relationship isn’t working, or their career is falling apart…

These kinds of destinations help with a couple of things:

Journey. Destination. Roadblock.

These are points of clarity in your client’s life that leads them to do healing work.

Journey. Destination. Roadblock.
These are points of clarity in your client’s life that leads them to do healing work.
Don’t mistake the mystical inner healing journey
with the context for the journey,
which are the external situations, goals, and destinations in one’s life.

But I also want you to be okay starting with vagueness.

Starting with Vagueness

For many of your clients, the most clearly they can articulate their destination is:
“I want it to work.”
That’s fine if you’re talking about a car, but it’s easier to be vague when

you’re discussing a business or a relationship.


As I urge you into picking destinations,
I also want you to make space to start with vagueness.

What if it was okay to be vague?
When I started my business, I started by selling single sessions, too.

What I don’t want you doing is living in vagueness.
If you start in vagueness,
I want you to bring an attitude of learning and exploration to your work with your clients


The Starting Point: Making It Up

Here’s another problem: if you are starting with vagueness, it doesn’t make for a very clear offer to your client. So you’re back at square one.

Guess what? You get to make it up.
That’s right, ply your business paint brush,
and just start splashing paint on the canvas, in a manner of speaking.

What I want you to do is to make your best, educated guess,
based on what you already know about your clients
and your area of expertise, and put it together.

Beyond the Mountain: One Last Bit About Destinations

You’re the expert.
Your client may have their sights fixed on getting to Disneyworld,
but as an expert, you know that there is much more beyond Disneyworld.

That means you end up with two different destinations.
There’s the destination the client wants for themselves,
and then there’s the destination that you want for them.

This is a distinction worth seeing,
because for many business owners,
you know that the client is going to be much happier when they reach the further destination.

For instance, your client may want to have a week of not arguing with their spouse,
but the destination you see is a relationship that truly inspires and nourishes both of them.

Your job is to show your clients that their destination and your destination for them
are not different paths
—but that your destination is simply a little further along the road than theirs.

What this might look like at first is to only acknowledge and focus on their destination.
If you don’t include their destination in how you present your offer,
they’ll believe you are forcing a choice on them: your destination or their destination.

Guess which one they’ll pick?

That’s right, they’ll leave you in the dust,
and continue trying to get to their destination.
Frustrating, watching them take off like that, isn’t it?
Especially when you know you can save them time and energy.

You’ve got to make it clear for them that it’s one journey.
Get clear on the second destination,
the one you see for them beyond where they are trying to go.
Then you can decide how and when it will work
to begin to unveil this larger picture to your clients.

Exercise | Choosing the Destination

Before getting to the details of your offer, first discover the destination.

1. Take a moment to drop into soul focus.
Let that deepen for a few breaths, and let go of concerns about “getting it right.”

2. Picturing a particular client of yours, what destination are they trying to reach?

3. In their journey towards this destination, what problem(s) are they running into?
What’s keeping them from reaching this destination?

4. Now ask in your heart to be shown, what is a further destination you want for them? What can you see beyond their destination?

Okay, so here are the destinations that will form the foundation of your offer. You now know where your client is trying to get to.
